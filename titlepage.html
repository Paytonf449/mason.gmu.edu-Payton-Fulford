Content

Through the incorporation of AI into cybersecurity strategies, organizations not only change the way they fight cyber threats but also reveal new vulnerabilities and raise issues of morality and ethics. This paper investigates the applications of AI in strengthening cyber defense, discussing its benefits, core legal and ethical issues, potential problems, and the need for more research in this area.

Legal & Ethical Issues

Bringing in AI in cybersecurity might show a way out from some problems, but it simultaneously gives rise to new legal and ethical issues. If AI algorithms use data as their training aid as well as their instrument for decision-making, questions about data privacy and consent arise (Schneier, 2015). While safeguarding the private and sensitive data for cybersecurity purposes, the processing and gathering of personal information can be done in accordance with international acts and principles like those of the European General Data Protection Regulation and the California Consumer Privacy Act (Schneier, 2015). On the other hand, in AI systems, bias algorithms raise ethical challenges by needing algorithmic systems that will disproportionately impact certain groups of people and also in the nature of achieving the unfair outcomes (Schneier, 2015). Organizations need to come up with a way of dealing with these issues by designing AI in a transparent manner and doing responsible AI development.

Security & Social Concerns

On the one hand, AI can be a great aid in cybersecurity, but it could also represent new challenges as well as a few social issues. Humanistic AI-powered cybersecurity systems serve as the target for adversarial attacks in which threat actors are purposely engineering AI algorithms to allow them to circumvent detection or inflict harm (Stallings & Brown, 2019). In addition to this, AI use for decision-making creates questions on how accountable such adoption is and whether there is trust in AI (Stallings & Brown, 2019). Poor governance over AI-directed cybersecurity programs may factor into a loss of trust and confidence in these technologies by the public if not well regulated (Stallings & Brown, 2019). Besides that, Europe faces issues with AI cybersecurity that could worsen the class inequalities if distributed access to advanced AI technologies is uneven (Stallings & Brown, 2019).

Further Required Research & Conclusion

For the sake of AI to stand on its full strength in cybersecurity and to tackle the underlying issues, thorough research is mandatory. This entails constructing powerful AI algorithms that can stand up to any adversarial attack or biased models, forging more precise legal and regulatory frameworks to govern AI-driven cybersecurity techniques resulting in the emergence of greater collaboration between industry, academia, and policymakers thereby encouraging responsible AI implementation (Stallings & Brown, 2019). AI has brought unparalleled opportunities for enhancing cybersecurity, the realization of it can only take place by the development of a thorough understanding of the legal as well as ethical and security implications and as we go on in our research the collaboration among disciplines is necessary (Zhang et al., 2020).
